Two Ladies Reading A Letter (This is said to be a copy of a post-card of the times.)
This is an interesting painting because it shows how Huston was borrowing from Dutch painters in the Golden Age of Dutch Painting, who in turn were influenced by Japanese woodblock prints.
See the illustrative triptych below:


"Two women reading a letter" by Kikugawa Eizan, "Sisters Reading a Book" by Dutch Golden Age of Danish Painting member Constantin Hansen and "Illicit Letter" by Italian Roccoco artist Vittorio Reggianini
When Japan reopened their trading ports with the West in 1854, Japanese art objects flooded into Europe. From the 1860s, ukiyo-e, Japanese woodblock prints, became a source of inspiration for many Western artists. Although the prints were sold domestically in Japan, Dutch merchants brought them to the west where they created a big impression on artists throughout Europe. The french coined the term "Japonisme" referring to the influence of Japanese art on Western art. Woodcuts, screens and porcelain flooded initially into France and the Netherlands. During the 1860s, these items, particularly the woodcuts, became a source of inspiration to many European Impressionist and post-Impressionist artists.
So here we can see an example of Japonsime travelling from its source in Japan, then to Holland, then to Italy and thence to Ontario, Canada.
